## The six frames (Ronald's own words, lightly tidied)

### Frame 1 — The AI transfer gap

> AI is amazing. Everyone talks about it. It's obvious you can do
> things with it — but HOW exactly? We already have products. We know
> AI is important. But we have no idea how to actually use it for
> ourselves. What does it bring? How do I build applications with AI
> as a customer? How do I turn this new technology into profit? What
> do I need to do? What's required? — Ronald delivers the know-how.

### Frame 2 — Keep your team at the cutting edge

> You have a great product. You have some marketing. But your
> employees can't keep up with technology in daily business. They're
> not at the pulse of the time. You want them to be. That's one of
> Ronald's business ideas.

### Frame 3 — Software Craftsmanship over mass-dev purchase

> Buying fifty cheap developers doesn't help. Software Craftsmen
> matter. Things should stay in-house — but you need the turbo boost.

### Frame 4 — Against the two-week consultant

> You've felt this: you book an expensive training or hire an
> expensive consultant. Two weeks of glow. Then you have the same
> problems as before. Ronald addresses exactly this — and establishes
> structures that sustain for years, not two weeks.

### Frame 5 — The pragmatic path (THE headline candidate)

> Be honest: when you go to a consultant, you know how it goes. A
> dying software project gets automated tests added, and great-
> sounding metrics like test coverage, analyzers, unit tests — they
> get stapled on. But if the kid has fallen in the well, it doesn't
> help. The pragmatic path has to be found. That's the headline: **The
> Pragmatic Path.** We only do things that deliver value — now or over
> time. We don't pile feature upon feature. We don't pile feature on
> feature.

### Frame 6 — Against the Scrum industry

> Buzzword bingo is great — Event Sourcing, AI, Scrum. But be honest:
> the Scrum industry inverts the idea. The real idea is the Agile
> Manifesto: **empower the team to seek and decide for itself, based
> on what the team actually found out.** The Scrum industry turns that
> on its head by pasting Schema-F over things that don't fit. **Schema-F
> Scrum is not agile.** And that's exactly the mistake made in Germany
> for forty years — not-agile teams.

(To sharpen: run the Agile-Manifesto research, then tighten this into
one or two line distillation.)

## The red thread / rote Faden

**"The Pragmatic Path" is the headline.**

Sub-beats, in customer-hero order:

1. You have a good product. Your team is capable. Something's off.
   (*Empathy — customer sees themselves*.)
2. The usual moves don't work: 50-dev outsourcing, 2-week consultants,
   Scrum-industry certifications, AI hype. (*Name the pain.*)
3. What actually works: empowerment, pragmatic structures that
   survive the quarter, know-how that stays in the team.
4. Ronald's role: turbo-boost + know-how transfer. Not replacement.
5. Specific territories where the transfer matters right now:
   - Turning AI from talk into working production systems.
   - Keeping the in-house team at the cutting edge.
   - Replacing buzzword-bingo architecture with pragmatic decisions.
6. How you work with Ronald — concrete formats (training, consulting,
   engineering) re-framed as *customer outcomes*, not *Ronald's
   offerings*.
7. Only then — who Ronald is, briefly, as proof (OSS, Recognized F#
   Expert, PXL Clock, F# Weekly, fsprojects). Deep-background — not
   the reason you're reading.
8. CTA that invites a conversation about the customer's problem, not
   a pitch about Ronald's calendar.

## Explicit anti-patterns to kill

- "I love F#" as hero copy.
- "Hire me" as CTA language.
- OSS star counts above the fold.
- Personal portrait above the fold (for at least several of the
  variants).
- Projects list as hero proof (they go further down, re-framed as
  evidence of craftsmanship).
- Any sentence that starts with "I" in the hero.
- Buzzword self-labelling ("full-stack AI consultant", "functional
  programming expert").
